INTRODUCTION
I was appointed District Electoral Areas Commissioner on 7 July 1992 lo review, in respect of each local government district, the grouping of local eavernment wards into
District Electoral Areas for the purpose of local vovernment elections, and te submit a
Report as soon as practicable setting out my recommendations on these groupings.
The Provisions concerning the appointment and function of the Commissioner, the
procedure he must follow in performing that function, and the rules in accordance with
which the recommendations of the Commissioner are to be made are contained in the
District Electoral Areas Commissioner (Northern Ireland) Order 1984 (hereinafter
referred to as the 1984 Order).
The Review was necessary in order to establish District Electoral Areas for the purpose of
local government elections, conducted by the single transferable vote system, following
the review of local government boundaries confirmed by the Local Government
(Boundaries) Order (Northern Ireland) 1992.
My task was to examine the number, boundaries and names of the existing District
Electoral Areas in relation to the district and ward boundaries confirmed in the Local
Government (Boundaries) Order (Northern Ireland) 1992; to review the available data:
to consider any representations made; and to report to the Secretary of State.
The Review was split into two stages, the first being the publication of my Provisional
Recommendations on 20 August 1992 inviting written representations or objections.
Consideration of the comments received, including oral representations at public
inquiries, led to the second and final stage, namely, the submission of this Report to the
Secretary of State setting out the Final Recommendations (Appendix J) together with the
associated set of maps (Appendix III).
The Commissioner may ask the Secretary of State to appoint one or more Assistant
Commissioners to inquire into and report to the Commissioner on such matters as the
Commissioner thinks fit and to assist with any public inquiries. At my request the
Secretary of State appointed Professor R H Buchanan, Mrs A Gibson, Professor N J
Gibson and Mr C Ward, who had all acted in a similar capacity for the Local Government
Boundaries Review.
I have also had the advice and support as Assessors of Mr P Bradley the Chief Electoral
Officer and Mr R McMurray, Registrar General of Births, Deaths and Marriages. In
addition my office has been staffed by a small group of civil servants seconded from the
Department of the Environment for Northern Ireland.

PRINCIPLES
| decided at the start of the Review to adhere as far as possible to the following five , 7
principles:
(a) minimal change generally to the existing DEA framework;
(h) establish as far as practicable distinct urban and rural DEAs;
(fc) seek to ensure electoral equality within districts by establishing the ratio of
electorate for each councillor within a +5% range when compared to the a
district electoral ratio per councillor;
(d) wards to be grouped to facilitate the representational work of councillors within any
district; and
(e} ease of voting for the electorate.
As Local Government Boundaries Commissioner I had sought to take account of the
physical size of districts; population; settlement patterns; topographical features and
special geographic situations (lakes, rivers, mountains); sense of local community; and
communication and transport links. I am satisfied that these factors are also important
when establishing DEA boundaries and I have endeavoured to ensure that my Final
Recommendations reflect a proper balance of all the various factors.
In framing my Final Recommendations I was also conscious of certain key observations
made by my predecessor Sir Frank Harrison in his final report namely:-
“... in designing the local government district and ward boundaries I sought to marry the
sense of local community identity with administrative practicality. In the Province the
local communities are small, but deeply sensitive of the social, geographical and other
factors which distinguish them from each other, and each local government district unit
has been made up of wards as the bricks in the structure. To construct an electoral area
which can also have a discernible community identity required, not only the grouping of
the right number of wards, but also the best possible selection to create electoral areas
readily identifiable by the electors within their bounds, and which would form local
government constituencies in which the differing sections of each community could
secure representation. In this present exercise, I endeavoured to secure as far as
practicable urban and rural electoral areas which were distinct from each other and at
the same time to avoid groupings which created an area “split” by major natural or
physical barriers. I also sought to create constituencies of manageable size and which
could be efficiently served by the elected representatives”.
While changes in electoral boundaries may well have the effect of changing the pattern 0”
political representation in subsequent elections this is not a matter of which the
Commissioner may take cognizance. Political arguments carried much less weight !
submissions based on the above principles .

Recommendations have been altered will reassure the public that the consultative
process was meaningful.
Iam most grateful for the advice and support of my Assessors. The assistance provided
hy the Director of Ordnance Survey and his staff, who have worked under pressure to
prepare the map series, has also been particularly helpful.
The Assistant Commissioners all of whom were involved in the various Public Inquiries
helped me greatly in coming to my final conclusions. Their co-operation and advice was
most useful and I am greatly indebted to them for their assistance.
I also wish to express my gratitude for the part played by my staff all of whom were
wholeheartedly dedicated to ensuring that the Review progressed efficiently and
effectively. Every staff member has willingly worked under pressure contributing beyond
what would normally be regarded as their ordinary duties in a sustained effort which
has enabled the Review to be completed so speedily. I must however pay particular
tribute to Mr Harry Baird who acted as Secretary to the Commissioner and Mr Trevor
Gordon as Chief Professional and Technical Officer. I wish them and all other staff
members every success in their future careers. I am also grateful to the Department of
the Environment (NI) for making staff of this calibre available to me.
Finally, I feel that I should highlight a difficulty which arose during the Review due to
the public perception of my role as DEAC in relation to my earlier appointment as Local
Government Boundaries Commissioner (LGBC). Both the public and public
representatives generally found it difficult to differentiate between the two Reviews.
While I am aware that the necessity for a two stage Review arises from the present
legislative provisions and the division of responsibility for local government boundaries
on the one hand and electoral arrangements on the other, between two Departments,
nevertheless | hope that before the next Review consideration might be given to some
consolidation of the procedure.
In my view the procedure relating to the establishment of local government boundaries
and district electoral area boundaries would be improved if both the DEAC and LGBC
Reviews were either combined in some way or at least dealt with in tandem. | believe that this would also ensure a much higher level of public participation and should also lead to a reduced timetable for the review of local government boundaries overall.
