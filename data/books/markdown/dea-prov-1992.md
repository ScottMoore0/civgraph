INTRODUCTION
The Secretary of State for Northern Ireland, Rt Hon Sir Patrick Mayhew, MP, appointed me as
District Electoral Areas Commissioner on 7 July 1992 to review, in respect of each local
government district, the grouping together of the local government wards into district
electoral areas for the purpose of local government elections and to submit a report as soon as
practicable setting out my recommendations on these groupings.
This appointment followed the submission to Government of my Final Recommendations as
Local Government Boundaries Commissioner, which were published on 15 June 1992 and
confirmed, without modifications, by the making of the Local Government (Boundaries)
Order (Northern Ireland) 1992 on 6 July 1992 (hereinafter referred to as the 1992 Order).
The Review is necessary in order to establish District Electoral Areas for the purpose of local
government elections conducted by the single transferable vote system of election.
The provisions concerning the appointment and function of the Commissioner, the
procedure he must follow in performing that function, and the rules in accordance with
which the recommendations of the Commissioner are to be made are contained in the
District Electoral Areas Commissioner (Northern Ireland) Order 1984 (hereinafter referred
to as the 1984 Order) (Appendix V).
The Office of the Commissioner is entirely independent and the Commissioner is not under
the direction of a Minister or of a Government Department.
The Commissioner’s task is to examine the number, boundaries and names of the existing
District Electoral Areas (DEA’s) in relation to the district and ward boundaries confirmed in
the 1992 Order; to review the available data; to consider any representations made to him;
and to report to the Secretary of State.
The Review can be split into two stages, the first being the publication of my Provisional
Recommendations inviting written representations or objections. This ts the stage
represented by the publication of this document together with the associated set of maps.
Consideration of the comments received including oral representations at any local public
hearings will lead to the second and final stage, namely, the submission of my Report to the
Secretary of State setting out the Final Recommendations.
The Commissioner may ask the Secretary of State to appoint one or more Assistant
Commissioners to inquire into and report to the Commissioner on such matters as the
Commissioner thinks fit and to assist with any public inquiries. At my request the Secretary of
State appointed Professor R H Buchanan, Mrs A Gibson, Professor N J Gibson and Mr C
Ward, who had all acted in a similar capacity for the Local Government Boundaries Review.
I have also had the advice and support, as Assessors, of Mr P Bradley the Chief Electoral
Officer and Mr R McMurray, Registrar General of Births, Deaths and Marriages. In addition
my office has been staffed by a small group of civil servants seconded from the Department of
the Environment for Northern Ireland,

BACKGROUND
The last Review was carried out during the period May-November 1984 and the ptesent
structure of 98 District Electoral Areas ( DEA’s) was established and confirmed in December
1984 following the submission of a Report to the Secretary of State by the then Gon missioner,
Sir Frank Harrison.
The 1984 Order requires that each DEA must consist of either five, six or seven local
government wards and each area returns as many council members as there are wards in the
area (Appendix III). At present there are 50 five-seater Areas, 20 six-seater Areas and 98
seven-seater Areas.
Although the recent review of the local government boundaries changed a number of district
and ward boundaries and created a net 16 additional wards, a number of local government
districts had only minimal ward boundary adjustments which has resulted in correspondingly
minimal change to the existing DEA boundaries im these districts (for example, Armagh,
Cookstown, Dungannon, Fermanagh).
Certain other districts experienced substantial ward boundary changes and although this has
led lo a corresponding substantial change in the exisung DEA boundaries nevertheless the
overall DEA structure for each of these districts has largely been retained (for example,
Belfast, Newry and Mourne, Craigavon and Derry).
The remaining districts have had major changes proposed to their existing DEA structures
largely due to the establishment of one or more additional wards within the district which has
in a small number of districts also necessitated the creation of an additional DEA for example,
Ards, Castlereagh, Coleraine and Lisburn.
The districts and wards on which these provisional recommendations are based are those
recently established by the 1992 Order.
The electoral base equates with that included in the Report of the Local Government
Boundaries Commissioner which recommended the new district and ward boundaries based on the February 199] Electoral Register,
The procedure which the Commissioner must follow involves the publication of Provisional
Recommendations which is represented through the publication of this document and the
associated maps (Appendix T}). Written representations are invited within one month from publication following which public hearings may be held to allow the opportunity for the presentation of further oral evidence probably during the period October/November 1992.
Finally, after considering all the representations made, the Commissioner must submiit a

The publication of these Provisional Recommendations provides the opportunity to argue for
either a substantially different grouping of Ue wards in any of the local government districts.
minimal change; or the continuation of the present arrangement. This can be done,
preferably in writing beforehand, or orally at local public hearings.
I now invite written comments on the Provisional Recommendations. It would be most
helpful if al] written representations indicate clearly the nature of any objection regarding
alterations and/or alternative ward groupings, explaining fully the reasons why [further
change is sought.
While changes in electoral boundaries may well have the effect of changing the pattern of
political representation in subsequent elections this is NOT a matter of which the
Commissioner may take cognizance. Political arguments, either written or oral, will carry
much less weight than submissions based on the various principles referred to earlier in this
Report, for example, the sense of local community.
Following receipt of written representations at this Provisional Stage public hearings will be
held, as appropniate, to give the opportunity for further oral representations on the written
comments received, probably during the period October to November 1992.
The hearings will give individuals the opportunity to comment on my Provisional
Recommendations, either to me personally or my Assistant Commissioners and to expand
their views on why further change is necessary, The hearings will be held in either town halls
or civic centres and will be conducted as informally as possible.

CONCLUSION
In preparing these Provisional Recommendations I was conscious of certain key observations
made by my predecessor Sir Frank Harrison in his final report namely:-
“. in designing the local government district and ward boundaries I sought to marry the
sense of local community identity with administrative practicality. In the Province the local
communities are small, but deeply sensiuve of the social, geographical and other factors which
distinguish them from each other, and each local government district unit has been made up
of wards as the bricks in the structure. To construct an electoral area which can also have a
discernible community identity required, not only the grouping of the right number of wards,
but also the best possible selection to create electoral areas readily identifiable by the electors
within their bounds, and which would form local government constituencies in which the
differing sections of each community could secure representauon. In this present exercise, [
endeavoured to secure as far as practicable urban and rural electoral areas which were distinct
from each other and at the same time to avoid groupings which created an area “split” by
major natural or physical barriers. I also sought to create constituencies of manageable size
and which could be efficiently served by the elected representatives’.
My intention has been to follow as near as possible these very sound ground rules established
in 1984,
In addition I have sought to keep the variation in the rato of electorate per councillor within
each district as small as possible. Ninety percent are within my self-imposed +5% range and
over half have been retained within a t2% range (para 4.1(¢)).
I am grateful to my staff, the Assessors and the Assistant Commissioners without whose help
and assistance the Provisional Recommendations could not have been prepared and
published within the ume available.
The Provisional Recommendations for each local government district are set out in Appendix
I and an associated set of maps outlining the recommendations is enclosed with this Report
The next stage of the Review will involve the detailed consideration of the written
representatious received prior to the holding of local public inquiries, as necessary.
